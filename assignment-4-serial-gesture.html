Assignment 4
Serial Gesture

For this assignment, the main goal was to create a serialized gesture using serial LEDs, buttons, sensors, sound, and physical interaction. I’ll basically say that this project was about taking the micro-gesture box and pushing it further by adding a new LED strip, 3D printed parts, keycaps, and a diffuser/enclosure so the project could show repeated motion and variation over time. The gesture becomes “serial” because the lights do not just turn on once; they repeat, shift, respond, and create a pattern based on the way the device is used. The sequence changes over time through the different inputs, like the buttons, sensors, and LED behavior. Each interaction creates a slightly different visual rhythm, which makes the documentation important because the photos show the physical build, while the video shows how the lights and movement actually perform. This project was not just about making the box work, but showing the process of testing, adjusting, and documenting how the hardware, software, and body interaction all connect.

Project Description

In this project, I worked on building a serial gesture device by adding an addressable LED strip to my micro-gesture box and combining it with buttons, sensors, and custom 3D printed parts. The assignment asked us to test serial LEDs, modify example code, create 3D printed keycaps, build or customize an LED strip mount with a diffuser, and then document how the final object worked through photos and video. The process involved a lot of testing because the LED strip had to be wired correctly, coded correctly, and placed onto the box in a way that made sense visually. I also had to think about how the repeated LED patterns could act like a gesture, not just decoration. The lights became a way to show rhythm, response, and motion, especially when the project was being handled or activated. What worked well was that the LED strip gave the box a stronger visual identity, and the 3D printed parts made it feel more like a finished object. What changed over time was how I understood the project, because at first it was mostly about getting the lights to work, but later it became more about how the lights, buttons, and movement could create a performative action. The final documentation shows the project as both an object and a process. The photos show the build, materials, and details, while the video shows the actual gesture happening through light, interaction, and movement.

Artist Statement

For this project, I wanted the serial gesture to feel like a small performance between the body, the box, and the LEDs. The repeated light patterns are not just there to decorate the object, but to show how a simple action can become something visual over time. Each button press and interaction creates a small change, and those changes start to feel like a rhythm or motion that keeps building. I’ll basically say that this piece is about turning a handmade electronic object into something that feels alive through repetition. The LEDs, keycaps, wires, and 3D printed parts all become part of the gesture, where the box responds back through color and movement. Since the lights repeat and shift, the project becomes less about one single moment and more about the process of testing, reacting, and performing with the object. The final video documentation shows that movement best because it captures how the project works through time, sound, and interaction instead of only existing as a still object.
